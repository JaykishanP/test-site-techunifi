Standard Take-Off


Techunifi Take off



Why?

  1. Cost Estimation: Accurate takeoffs ensure projects stay within budget.
  2. Time Management: Facilitates timely ordering of materials and alignment with deadlines.
  3. Resource Allocation: Ensures efficient allocation of materials and labor.
  4. Quality Control: Provides a detailed breakdown of materials and quantities to meet specifications.

In short, a takeoff is a vital project management process that translates design into actionable steps, ensuring successful execution.

Step 1 - Review Drawings:

Blueprints show a commercial office building with:

  • 50 network outlets.
  • 5 security cameras.
  • A centralized server room.

Step 2 - Identify and Mark the Components:

Systems and Equipment:

Mark the locations of low-voltage equipment like cameras, access control panels, speakers, network switches, routers, alarm panels, fiber optic cables, and Power over Ethernet (PoE) lines.

Cabling:

Identify and measure cable routes, specifying whether they are Cat5e, Cat6, fiber optic, or other cable types used in the system.

Conduit and Pathways:

Measure the lengths and pathways where conduits or raceways will carry cables.

Termination Points:

Identify where the cables terminate (e.g., patch panels, network switches, or other devices).

Step 3 - Measure Quantities from the Drawings:

Linear Measurement:

Measure the total cable length required by following the paths in the drawings. Use a scale ruler or digital measuring tool for accuracy.

Count Devices:

Count the number of devices to be installed, such as cameras, sensors, speakers, network outlets, or access control points.

Conduit and Raceway:

Measure the total length of conduit needed to enclose cables and wiring.

Step 4 - Note the Specifications for Each Item:

Cable Type and Size:

Specify the type (e.g., Cat6, fiber optic) and gauge (e.g., 18/2, 24 AWG) of cables.

Device Specifications:

Note the model, type, voltage, and other relevant details for each device (e.g., CCTV camera, network switch).

Special Requirements:

Identify any unique installations, such as ceiling-mounted cameras, wall-mounted access control panels, or custom network racks.

Step 5 - Measure Quantities from the Drawings:

Material List:

Prepare a bill of materials (BOM) with quantities for all components, including cables, connectors, and devices.

Cost Estimate:

Estimate the costs of materials and labor by multiplying quantities by the unit prices from suppliers.

Labor and Time Estimate:

Provide labor estimates, including time for cable pulls, device installation, and system testing.

Step 6 - Review for Accuracy:

  • Double-check all measurements, quantities, and material specifications to ensure completeness.
  • Cross-reference the takeoff with project specifications to confirm alignment with the design intent.

Step 7 - Prepare for Procurement and Installation:

  • The takeoff will guide procurement teams in ordering the correct materials.
  • Installation teams will use the takeoff as a blueprint for on-site work.

Example of a Takeoff for a Low-Voltage Network System

1. Review Drawings:

  • Blueprints show a commercial office building with:
    • 50 network outlets.
    • 5 security cameras.
    • A centralized server room.

2. Mark Components:

  • Network outlets (RJ45) at desks and workstations.
  • CCTV cameras in hallways and entrances.
  • Fiber optic cables for a high-speed network backbone between server rooms and closets.

3. Measurement:

  • Cabling:
    • 50 RJ45 outlets = 1,500 feet of Cat6 cable (each outlet requires about 30 feet).
    • 5 CCTV cameras = 1,000 feet of coaxial cable.
    • 2 fiber optic runs = 2,000 feet of single-mode fiber.
  • Devices:
    • 5 cameras, each requiring 1 mounting bracket and 1 PoE injector.
    • 2 network switches, each requiring rack-mounted hardware.

4. Specifications:

  • Cable: Cat6 for networking, fiber optic for server room connections, and coaxial for CCTV.
  • Devices:
    • 5 PoE cameras (10W each).
    • 2 48-port network switches.
    • 5 RJ45 jacks.

5. Create Takeoff Report:

  • Material List:
    • 1,500 feet of Cat6 cable.
    • 1,000 feet of coaxial cable.
    • 2,000 feet of fiber optic cable.
    • 5 PoE security cameras.
    • 2 network switches.
  • Labor Estimate:
    • 2 days for cable pulling.
    • 1 day for camera installation.
    • 1 day for network switch installation and configuration.

Tools for Takeoff

  • Scale Ruler or Digital Tools: For measuring distances and dimensions on drawings.
  • Takeoff Software: Tools like Bluebeam, PlanSwift, or Procore for automating processes.
  • Estimating Software: Programs like Estimating Edge or Sage Estimating for cost calculations.
  • Blueprints: Paper or digital versions of construction drawings.
  • Spreadsheets: For organizing material lists, labor costs, and estimates.
